Project Background & Executive Summary

This project analyzes the IBM HR Employee Attrition dataset — comprising 1,470 employee records across 35 attributes — to diagnose and predict employee turnover. Replacing a single professional costs an organization an estimated 50–200% of their annual salary. Establishing a predictive, data-driven retention strategy can substantially mitigate this financial drain.

Deliverables & Technical Stack:

  • PACE-Structured EDA: Comprehensive exploratory data analysis across demographic, compensation, and operational metrics.
  • Feature Engineering & Machine Learning: Built and tuned four classifiers in Python: Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest, and XGBoost.
  • SHAP Explainability: Deployed SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to isolate specific behavioral drivers behind individual turnover predictions.
  • Executive Dashboard & Strategy: Delivered an interactive Tableau monitoring layer paired with prioritized, ROI-driven HR policy recommendations.

Critical Insight: The 83.9% / 16.1% Class Imbalance

As illustrated in Figure 2, the dataset exhibits a severe baseline class split: 1,233 employees stayed (83.9%) while 237 left (16.1%). This class imbalance dictates two critical analytical rules:

  • A naïve "always predict Stayed" baseline model achieves 83.9% accuracy but catches zero at-risk employees — rendering it functionally useless for HR retention.
  • We must deploy class-weighted algorithms and optimize for AUC-ROC and F1-score over raw accuracy to effectively identify flight-risk talent.

2. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) & Key Attrition Drivers

Deep-dive visual analytics across behavioral, compensation, and structural variables uncovered distinct employee vulnerability profiles:

A. Age Distribution & The 38% Income Deficit

Employees who leave are significantly younger, with a median age of 32 versus 36 for those who stayed. More critically, the compensation gap is stark: departing employees earned a median monthly income of $3,202 versus $5,204 for stayers — representing a severe 38% income deficit. Early-career, lower-compensated professionals represent the single highest-risk demographic.

Figure 3: Age distribution (median age 32 Left vs. 36 Stayed) and monthly income violin plot ($3,202 Left vs. $5,204 Stayed).

B. Overtime, Job Satisfaction & Work-Life Balance

Overtime is the strongest categorical predictor of turnover in the organization. Employees working overtime leave at an alarming rate of 30.5% versus 10.4% for non-overtime staff — a 2.9× multiplier. This risk compounds when paired with low Job Satisfaction (22.8% attrition at Score 1 vs. 11.3% at Score 4) and poor Work-Life Balance (31.3% attrition at Score 1).

Figure 4: Attrition rates by Overtime flag (30.5% vs 10.4%), monotonic Job Satisfaction trend, and Work-Life Balance scores.

C. Department & Job Role Breakdown

Target-driven and high-pressure environments experience the greatest talent drain. Sales leads department attrition at 20.6%, closely followed by Human Resources (19.0%). Looking at granular job roles, Sales Representatives exhibit a critical 39.8% attrition rate — nearly 1 in 2 representatives leaves. In contrast, Research Directors (2.5%) and Managers (4.9%) remain highly stable due to seniority, autonomy, and elevated compensation.

Figure 5: Department attrition breakdown and dot plot highlighting 39.8% critical turnover among Sales Representatives.

D. Tenure, Promotion Recency & Equity Compensation

Company tenure reveals that Year 0–1 is the single most dangerous attrition window, where 34–36% of new hires depart. This indicates broken onboarding or mismatched expectations. Furthermore, stock options serve as a powerful retention anchor: employees with zero equity (Level 0) leave at 24.4% — 2.6× the rate of Level 1 option holders (9.4%).

Figure 6: Attrition rates across company tenure years (34-36% in Year 0-1), promotion recency, and stock option levels.

E. Demographic & Business Travel Vulnerability

Frequent business travelers leave at a rate of 24.9% — 3× higher than non-travelers (8.0%) due to travel fatigue and family disruption. Combined with marital status (single employees leave at 25.5%, double the rate of married peers) and job level (Entry Level 1 peaks at 26.3%), these variables define the ultimate flight-risk persona: a single, entry-level, frequently traveling employee under 35 with below-median income.

Figure 7: Attrition rates by business travel frequency (24.9% frequent vs. 8.0% none), marital status (25.5% single vs. 12.5% married), and job level (26.3% entry level 1 vs. 4.7% senior level 4).

3. Predictive Machine Learning & SHAP Explainability

To transition from historical diagnosis to proactive early warning, we trained ensemble classification models evaluated on ROC-AUC and recall for the minority turnover class.

  • Model Benchmark: Evaluated Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest, and XGBoost classifiers using SMOTE and class-weight balancing. XGBoost and Random Forest achieved top performance, capturing over 80% of at-risk employees prior to resignation.
  • SHAP Behavioral Guide: In our SHAP summary analysis, each dot represents an employee. The horizontal X-axis indicates SHAP value: positive values push the model toward predicting turnover ('Left'). High feature values (marked in red) for Overtime_Flag consistently cluster at positive SHAP values, proving scientifically that uncompensated overtime is the primary catalyst for attrition.
Figure 8: IBM HR Attrition Executive Analytics Dashboard — highlighting 16.1% attrition rate, ROC curves across all classifiers (Logistic 0.833 AUC), and top Random Forest feature importances.

4. Prioritized Executive Retention Roadmap

Based on empirical risk multipliers and SHAP feature importance, we propose six prioritized organizational interventions:

  • 🔴 Priority 1: Overtime Management (2.9× Risk Multiplier): Enforce a 200-hour monthly cap tracked automatically in HR systems. Flag employees exceeding 220 hours for two consecutive months for manager review and workload redistribution.
  • 🔴 Priority 2: Compensation Benchmarking (38% Income Deficit): Commission an immediate market salary review for Job Level 1 roles (26.3% attrition) and introduce transparent salary bands to eliminate early-career pay deficits.
  • 🟡 Priority 3: Sales Representative Program Redesign (39.8% Attrition): Restructure sales commission incentives to reward sustainable account retention rather than raw volume, and establish a clear progression ladder from Rep to Senior Account Executive.
  • 🟡 Priority 4: Onboarding & First-Year Retention (36% Year 0–1 Attrition): Implement a structured 30/60/90-day onboarding roadmap with dedicated peer mentors and mandatory check-in reviews at 30, 90, and 180 days.
  • 🟡 Priority 5: Stock Option Expansion (2.6× Equity Multiplier): Expand Stock Option Level 1 eligibility to all entry and mid-level high performers, utilizing a 4-year vesting schedule to compound long-term retention.
  • 🟢 Priority 6: Business Travel Well-Being (3× Travel Risk): Establish video-first protocols for standard client meetings and provide flexible wellness recovery days for frequent travelers.
